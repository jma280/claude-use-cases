# Reusable Prompt: Build a Swim Lesson Plan

Copy everything in the box below into Claude or another AI assistant. Fill in the bracketed fields with your own details first. The more you fill in, the better the plan.

---

```
You are an experienced swim instructor building a day-by-day learn-to-swim
plan for a parent or caregiver to run themselves. Use the details below.

ABOUT THE SWIMMER
- Age: [e.g., 2 years 3 months / 5 years / 9 years / adult]
- Current ability: [e.g., none and fearful / comfortable playing but won't put
  face in / can float and kick / can swim one stroke a short distance]
- Fear, sensory, developmental, or medical notes: [e.g., scared of going under,
  sensory sensitivity to water on the face, a disability or condition to account
  for, or "none"]

THE SCHEDULE
- Start date: [date]
- Target end date: [date]
- Days per week / which days: [e.g., daily, or Mon/Wed/Fri]
- Max session length: [e.g., 30 minutes]
- Any gaps with no water access: [e.g., traveling July 16 to 21, no pool, or "none"]

THE SETTING
- Water access: [e.g., YMCA pool with a shallow shelf / backyard pool / lake or
  beach], depth and temperature if known
- Equipment available: [e.g., dive rings, kickboard, none]

WHO IS TEACHING
- Relationship and swim ability: [e.g., parent, strong swimmer who could rescue
  the child / parent, weak swimmer]

MY GOAL IN MY OWN WORDS
- [e.g., "I want her swimming by the end of the month" or "I want him water-safe
  before our beach trip"]

INSTRUCTIONS FOR YOU:

1. Set a realistic goal for the swimmer's AGE and current ability first. If my
   stated goal is unrealistic for the age, tell me directly and reset it. Do not
   tell me what I want to hear. Use the right framework for the age:
   - Toddlers (roughly 1 to 3): water competency and self-rescue, not swimming.
     The headline skills are the back float, breath control on cue, and a
     turn-back-to-the-wall reflex. Strokes are not developmentally realistic yet.
   - Preschool (roughly 4 to 5): comfort plus the beginnings of independent
     movement. Glides, kicking, short unassisted swims, back float to breathe.
   - School age and up: stroke technique, breathing, endurance, and deeper-water
     safety.
   Adjust for the specific child, not just the age band.

2. Bake in these safety non-negotiables and state them in the plan:
   - Touch supervision the whole time. No plan drown-proofs anyone.
   - No goggles for self-rescue practice, so the skill works in a real fall.
   - No floaties or puddle jumpers during lessons. They teach the upright
     "drowning position." Open play under supervision is different.
   - Never surprise-submerge a child. Always use a verbal cue first.
   - The teacher must be able to swim and rescue the swimmer. If not, say so.
   - The teacher's calm sets the child's calm. Fear transmits.

3. Account for my schedule exactly. Map the plan to my real dates and frequency.
   For any no-water gap, give a maintenance routine (for example, bathtub back
   float practice) so progress holds.

4. Prevent burnout. Build in lighter "favorites only" days at a sensible rhythm,
   keep sessions short for young kids, and end every session on a win.

5. Use consistent verbal cues taught the same way every time, and list them.

6. Structure each session the same way: short warmup, the day's skill broken up
   with a game, then free play and a win to finish.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- The realistic goal for this swimmer, stated plainly, and how it differs from
  my stated goal if it does
- A "how to use this" section with the session template, the safety
  non-negotiables, and the cue glossary
- A day-by-day plan mapped to my dates, each day with a focus, the warmup, the
  main skill with cues, a game, and the finishing win
- A maintenance routine for any gaps
- "What success looks like" at the end date, in concrete checkable skills
- "What this will not achieve," so my expectations are honest
- When to consider a certified instructor or a survival-swim program instead of
  or in addition to this

Ground the plan in current water-safety guidance where you can. Note clearly
that this does not replace certified instruction, and that real survival-swim
self-rescue is taught by trained professionals.
```

---

## Tips for better results

- Be honest about current ability. "Comfortable but won't put her face in" produces a very different plan than "can already float."
- If you are not a confident swimmer yourself, say so. The plan should change, and for very young or fearful swimmers a certified instructor may be the safer call.
- Fewer, consistent days beat cramming. Tell it your real cadence, not your ideal one.
- After it generates the plan, you can push back: "she is more advanced than that," "make the sessions 20 minutes," "she is terrified of going under, slow it down." It will adjust.
